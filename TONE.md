# RoleOS — tone & voice

Single source of truth for how RoleOS talks — on the landing page, in the chatbot, in the product UI, in waitlist emails, everywhere. Apply this anywhere a human will read words we wrote.

---

## Who we're talking to

A senior PM, TPM, BizOps lead, or program manager currently job-searching at AI-native companies. They are:

- **Smart.** They've shipped products. They know what archetype, must-have, and visa policy mean.
- **Time-poor.** They're juggling a current role (or a recent layoff), recruiter calls, applications, and their actual life. They scan, they don't read.
- **Often anxious.** Job-searching is emotionally loaded — even for senior people. Maybe especially for senior people.
- **Skeptical of pitches.** They've seen every B2B SaaS landing page. They can spot performative copy in three seconds.

**What they need from us:** a calm, capable ally — not another product pitch.

---

## Three words

**Calm. Respectful. Useful.**

If a sentence doesn't land at least one of these, rewrite it.

---

## Five principles

### 1. Assume intelligence
They're senior. They know what these words mean. Don't over-explain, don't define obvious terms, don't talk down. Trust them to read fast.

### 2. Acknowledge the work
Job search is exhausting. Copy should feel like a friend handing you a coffee, not a salesperson handing you a pitch deck. We never imply they're doing it wrong. We help them do it better.

### 3. Show, don't flex
Numbers do the work. "We read 458 JDs" lands harder than "We crushed the JD-reading game." If we have to flex, we don't really have the goods.

### 4. Helpful, not heroic
RO assists. The user is still the protagonist of their job search. No "we've cracked it" / "we've solved it" / "we've reinvented" energy.

### 5. Quiet confidence
Say it once, plainly. Don't stack adjectives. Don't repeat for emphasis. Don't lean on **bold** and *italics* for impact — they should be rare and load-bearing when used.

## On the chatbot (RO)

- Speak in the first person as RO.
- Acknowledge the question briefly, then answer.
- "I haven't seen that in the data" beats "Sorry, I can't help with that."
- Never apologize for things that aren't apologies. No "Sorry but…".
- Use em-dashes for natural pauses. Avoid semicolons unless they really fit.
- Reply length: 2-4 sentences for most answers. Longer only when the user asks for depth.

---

## On the FAQ

- Lead each answer with the answer, not a preamble.
- Avoid "Great question" / "Excellent point" / "Hang tight" / "Stay tuned."
- If the answer is no, say no, then explain in one sentence.
- If the answer is "soon" or "in beta," say so honestly.

---

## On microcopy & error states

- "Something went wrong" beats "Oops!" — calm, not cute.
- "We'll let you know" beats "Stay tuned!"
- Never use exclamation marks. Even in success states. The job is too serious.
- "You're on the list. We'll be in touch." beats "Yay, you're in!"

---

## Banned words & phrases

Never write these:

- Exclamation marks (`!`)
- "As an AI" (in chatbot)
- "Awesome" / "Great question" / "Hang tight" / "Stay tuned"
- "Sorry but…" (it's not a real apology)
- "Game-changer" / "10x" / "crush it" / "level up" / "unlock"
- "Revolutionary" / "industry-leading" / "best-in-class" / "world-class"
- "Effortless" / "frictionless" / "seamless" (overused, dishonest — nothing is)
- "Just" (as in "just drop your email" — apologizing for the ask)

---

## The one-line test

Before any copy ships, read it aloud and ask:

> *Would a calm, knowledgeable friend say this to a senior PM who's deep in a job search at 11pm on a Tuesday?*

If it sounds salesy, hype-y, hedged, performative, or like a typical SaaS landing page — rewrite it.

---

## Spelling & formatting

- American English. (organize, color, behavior, recognized)
- Em-dashes (—) for natural pauses. Spaced em-dashes are fine.
- Sentence case for headlines and buttons. ("Join the waitlist" not "Join The Waitlist".)
- No Oxford comma unless removing it creates ambiguity.
- Numerals over words for 10 and above. ("8 questions" / "458 roles".)

---

## When in doubt

Cut a word. Then cut another one. Then read it aloud.
